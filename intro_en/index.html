    1. Introduction

    For millennia, humanity has observed phenomena it did not understand and often interpreted them as signs or acts of the divine. However, over the centuries, science has advanced, providing explanations where once there were only mysteries and beliefs. For instance, during the Middle Ages, the Black Death was seen as a divine punishment. Diseases were interpreted as responses to sins or as tests of faith. Today, we know that microbes, viruses, and parasites are responsible for some of these illnesses. Others are linked to genetics. Medicine has made great strides in understanding their origins and developing treatments.

    In the past, an eclipse was viewed as an omen or a sign from the gods. Now, astronomy can precisely predict when and where an eclipse will occur, thanks to our understanding of orbits and planetary alignments.

    Is it not wise, then, to ask what still belongs to the realm of the divine?

    Certainly, science has helped explain much of what was once mysterious, especially questions related to “how,” such as:

    • How stars are formed,
    • How diseases spread,
    • How species evolve over time.

    But some questions remain open, particularly those that touch on “why,” such as:

    • Why does the universe exist?
    • Why does consciousness emerge from matter?
    • Why is there something rather than nothing?

    This text is addressed to those who, like me, seek a form of spirituality - a belief that can coexist with reason. I believe that spirituality and science/reason are not antagonistic.

    “A little science takes you away from God, but a lot brings you back.” - Louis Pasteur.

    “Anyone who is seriously involved in the pursuit of science becomes convinced that a spirit is manifest in the laws of the universe - a spirit vastly superior to that of man, and one in the face of which we with our modest powers must feel humble.” - Albert Einstein.
